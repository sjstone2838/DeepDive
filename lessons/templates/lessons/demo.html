Goal: Understand what a leveraged buyout (LBO) model is, and what it is used for

What is a Leveraged Buyout Model?

  • Buyout refers to the purchase of a company – more generally, it stands for an investment of money
  • Leveraged refers to the purchase of a company using loans (debt) as well as cash (equity)
    • Similar to purchasing a $100,000 house with $50,000 from the bank as a loan
  • A financial model is a tool used to analyze and project a company’s performance

What does an LBO help me do?

  • Simply, determine whether an investment is projected to be a good one, and how good
  • Understand what break points are for decision – what could change the outcome of the investment?
  • LBO can be used to analyze return on any type of investment being made
    • Private equity industry
    • Film financing
    • Buying a home

Goal: Understand how to pay for a company and where the money from the purchase goes

Sources & Uses

  • Sources refers to where the money for a deal is coming from
    • A $100,000 house can be paid for with $50,000 in your own money and $50,000 from a loan from the bank
    • Example above is 50% equity and 50% debt
  • Uses refers to where the money from a deal is going to
    • Person who sold the house might have $40,000 left on her mortgage
    • In that case, the seller herself is getting $60,000 while she is using $40,000 to pay off her remaining mortgage

Home Example

  • Imagine the prior example of buying a house that costs $100,000
    • You decide to take out a loan for $40,000 to pay for the house
    • The remaining $60,000 is from cash you already have
  • In this case, “Enterprise Value” of the house is $100,000
    • “Debt” is $40,000 or 40% of the Enterprise Value
    • “Equity” is $60,000 or 60% of the Enterprise Value

Home Example, Continued

  • Now imagine the seller of that $100,000 house
    • She has $20,000 left on her mortgage
    • She will be able to keep the remaining $80,000
  • In this example, Enterprise Value of the house is $100,000 (same as before)
    • Debt is $20,000 or 20% of the Enterprise Value
    • Equity is $80,000 or 80% of the Enterprise Value
  • Notice that Enterprise Value was the same but the Debt & Equity mix was different between buyer and seller

Lesson Hints
  • Total Enterprise Value = Equity + Debt - Cash
  • Sources of Cash (from Seller) = Uses of Cash (to Buyer)
  • Buyer Debt + Buyer Equity = Seller Debt (paid down) + Seller Equity (e.g. proceeds from sale)
